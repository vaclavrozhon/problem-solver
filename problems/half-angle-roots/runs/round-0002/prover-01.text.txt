Ideas
- Use the sine-product expression at v=e^{π i/(2n)} to reduce each summand to an exponential scale via Euler–Maclaurin. This gives a rate function Φ(α) whose maximizer determines the exponential growth.
- Prove that the unique maximizer is at α⋆=1/3 and evaluate Φ(α⋆) in terms of the Lobachevsky/Clausen function, matching v_3=Vol(regular ideal tetrahedron)=Cl_2(π/3)=3Λ(π/3) and V(4_1)=2v_3.
- Use positivity of the summands to sandwich f_n between its maximal term and (n+1) times the maximal term, yielding an O(log n) error and the sharp exponent (V/π)n.

Sine representation and positivity (rigorous)
Let θ:=π/(2n). Then [m]_v=sin(mθ)/sin θ and, for 0≤i≤n,
  
  C_{n,i}:=\qbinom{n+i}{2i}_v=∏_{k=n-i+1}^{n+i} \frac{\sin(kθ)}{\sin(kθ)}\Big/∏_{k=1}^{2i}\sin(kθ)=\frac{∏_{k=n-i+1}^{n+i}\sin(kθ)}{∏_{k=1}^{2i}\sin(kθ)}.
  
Equivalently, using sin(π/2±x)=cos x,
  
  C_{n,i}=\frac{\cos(iθ)\prod_{k=1}^{i-1}\cos^2(kθ)}{\prod_{k=1}^{2i}\sin(kθ)}.
  
Hence C_{n,i}>0 for 0≤i≤n−1, and C_{n,n}=1 as a rational function in v (limit interpretation at k=2n).

Uniform Euler–Maclaurin for log-sine sums (Lemma A)
Define S(m):=∑_{k=1}^m \log(2\sin(kθ)) for 1≤m≤2n−1. Then, uniformly for m in [1,2n−1],
  
  S(m)=\frac{1}{θ}\,Λ(mθ) + E(m),\quad Λ(x):=−\int_0^{x}\log(2\sin u)\,du,\quad E(m)=O(\log n).
  
Sketch of proof: Apply one-step Euler–Maclaurin to f(u)=\log(2\sin u) on [θ,mθ]; the derivative f′(u)=\cot u has integrable singularities, and ∫_{θ}^{mθ}|\cot u|du≲\log\frac{1}{\sin θ}+\log\frac{1}{\sin(\min\{mθ,\pi−mθ\})}=O(\log n). The endpoint half-sum terms are O(1). This yields E(m)=O(\log n), uniformly in m.

Rate function and its maximizer (Lemma B)
For i=⌊α n⌋ with α∈[0,1], combining S(n+i), S(n−i), S(2i) and Lemma A gives
  
  \log C_{n,i}=\frac{2n}{\pi}J(\alpha)+O(\log n),\quad J(\alpha)=\int_{\tfrac{\pi}{2}(1−\alpha)}^{\tfrac{\pi}{2}(1+\alpha)}\!\log\sin u\,du\;−\int_{0}^{\pi\alpha}\!\log\sin u\,du.
  
Equivalently, with Λ as above,
  
  Φ(\alpha):=\frac{1}{\pi}[Λ(\tfrac{\pi}{2}(1−\alpha)) + Λ(\pi\alpha) − Λ(\tfrac{\pi}{2}(1+\alpha))],\quad \log C_{n,i}=2n\,Φ(\alpha)+O(\log n),
  
uniformly for α in compact subsets of (0,1). Differentiating using Λ′(x)=−\log(2\sin x):
  
  Φ′(\alpha)=\log\frac{\cos(\tfrac{\pi\alpha}{2})}{\sin(\pi\alpha)},\quad Φ″(\alpha)=−\frac{\pi}{2}\tan\frac{\pi\alpha}{2}−\pi\cot(\pi\alpha).
  
Thus Φ′(\alpha)=0 iff \cos(\tfrac{\pi\alpha}{2})=\sin(\pi\alpha)=2\sin(\tfrac{\pi\alpha}{2})\cos(\tfrac{\pi\alpha}{2}), i.e. \sin(\tfrac{\pi\alpha}{2})=\tfrac12, giving the unique critical point α⋆=1/3. Since Φ″(\alpha)<0 on (0,1/2), in particular Φ″(1/3)=−\tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{2}<0, α⋆ is the unique global maximizer.

Exact value at the maximizer (Lemma C)
Set x=\tfrac{\pi}{6}. Using F(x):=Λ(2x)+2Λ(\tfrac{\pi}{2}−x), we have Φ(1/3)=(1/\pi)F(\pi/6) and F(\pi/6)=Λ(\pi/3)+2Λ(\pi/3)=3Λ(\pi/3).
Classically, the volume v_3 of the regular ideal tetrahedron equals v_3=Cl_2(\pi/3)=3Λ(\pi/3), and the figure-eight volume is V=2v_3. Hence
  
  Φ(1/3)=\frac{v_3}{\pi}=\frac{V}{2\pi}.
  
References: Milnor (Hyperbolic geometry), Neumann–Zagier (Volumes of hyperbolic three-manifolds), Lewin (Polylogarithms), using Cl_2(2x)=2Λ(x) and the identity 2Cl_2(\pi/3)=3Cl_2(2\pi/3).

Asymptotics for f_n (Proposition)
For all i, \log C_{n,i}=2n Φ(i/n)+O(\log n). Thus
  
  \max_{0≤i≤n}\log C_{n,i}=2n\max_{α∈[0,1]}Φ(α)+O(\log n)=2n Φ(1/3)+O(\log n)=\frac{V}{\pi}n+O(\log n).
  
Since C_{n,i}≥0, we have
  
  \max_i C_{n,i} ≤ f_n(e^{\pi i/n}) ≤ (n+1)\max_i C_{n,i},
  
so
  
  \log f_n(e^{\pi i/n})=\frac{V}{\pi}n+O(\log n).
  
In particular, the exponential growth rate is V/π, not V/(2π).

Ratio test and unimodality (auxiliary, optional)
An exact ratio is
  
  R_n(i):=\frac{C_{n,i+1}}{C_{n,i}}=\frac{\sin((n−i)θ)\sin((n+i+1)θ)}{\sin((2i+1)θ)\sin((2i+2)θ)}=\frac{\cos(iθ)\cos((i+1)θ)}{\sin((2i+1)θ)\sin((2i+2)θ)}.
  
For i=⌊α n⌋, R_n(i)=\exp(Φ′(\alpha)+o(1)), so R_n(i)>1 for α<1/3 and R_n(i)<1 for α>1/3 for n large, implying unimodality with peak near i≈n/3. This is not needed for the O(log n) precision above but useful for local limit refinements.

Numerical check (suggested)
Compute a_n:=(1/n)\log f_n(e^{\pi i/n}). Then a_n→V/π≈0.64626. Even modest n (e.g. n=200,400) should exhibit close agreement.
