Foundational corrections (local topology facts)
- Cylinders are clopen. For any realized r‑ball F, the cylinder G_•^F = {(G,ρ): B_r(G,ρ) ≅ F} is open by definition, and closed because if B_r(x_n) ≅ F for all n and x_n → x in d_•, then for each s ≤ r we eventually have B_s(x_n) ≅ B_s(x), hence B_r(x) ≅ F. This implies a practical criterion: S ⊂ G_• is nowhere dense if and only if for every realized r‑ball F there exists a realized r′‑ball F′ with G_•^{F′} ⊆ G_•^{F} and G_•^{F′} ∩ S = ∅. The current Lemma 13 should be corrected to use closure (or be restricted to clopen bases, where closure equals the set itself).
- Nonemptiness of cylinders in G_{•,≤D}. If F is a realized r‑ball in G_{•,≤D}, then some boundary vertex v ∈ L_r(F) has deg_F(v) < D; otherwise no outside neighbor can be added and the graph cannot be infinite. The text currently states a sufficient condition (“contains a vertex of degree < D at distance r”); for realized balls it is necessary and sufficient.

Rigorous bridge forcing in ≤ D (all D ≥ 2)
Lemma (saturated bubble, ≤ D). Fix D ≥ 2. For any realized r‑ball F and any boundary vertex u ∈ L_r(F) with deg_F(u) ≤ D − 1, there exist R > r and a realized R‑ball F′ extending F such that for every (G,ρ) ∈ G_{•,≤D}^{F′} the graph G has a 1‑way infinite bridge e incident with u.
Proof. Construct a finite “bubble” H disjoint from B_r(F) with a designated vertex w. Add a single new edge e = uw. Inside H, add edges and new vertices (all beyond radius r) so that:
- Every vertex of H except w has degree exactly D within the induced subgraph on B_R(G,ρ) (so no spare stubs remain inside the radius R), and w has degree D − 1 inside H; after adding e, w also has degree D within B_R.
- No vertex of H lies at distance R from ρ; equivalently, all vertices of H have their full degree D within the ball, so in any extension with the same R‑ball they cannot receive edges from outside without changing B_R.
Then in any extension with B_R = F′, there is exactly one edge across the cut between H and the rest, namely e; therefore e is a bridge. The H‑side is finite. To ensure G_{•,≤D}^{F′} is nonempty, leave at least one vertex on the boundary L_R(F′) with residual degree (< D) so that infinite completions exist. QED.
Remarks. The pendant‑leaf construction used in the current text is not robust: in extensions, new edges could attach to the “leaf” and destroy bridgeness. Saturating all vertices on the bubble‑side within the fixed ball remedies this.

Odd D regular: forcing a bridge in G_{•,=D}
Lemma (saturated bubble, = D, odd D ≥ 3). Let D be odd. For any realized r‑ball F in G_{•,=D} and any u ∈ L_r(F), there exist R and a realized R‑ball F′ extending F such that every (G,ρ) ∈ G_{•,=D}^{F′} has a 1‑way infinite bridge.
Proof idea. As above, build a finite bubble H and place all its vertices at radius < R. Ensure that in F′ every vertex of H has total degree D within B_R after adding e = uw; hence no bubble‑side vertex can receive edges from outside in any extension with the same R‑ball. On the core‑side, do not saturate all boundary vertices, so there are D‑regular infinite completions. Because D is odd, parity does not forbid 1‑way bridges. Existence of D‑regular infinite completions can be ensured explicitly by attaching an infinite D‑regular tree outside along the remaining boundary stubs. The quantifiers must be written carefully exactly as in the ≤ D case; the key is that all bubble‑side degrees equal D already within the radius R.
Parity obstruction for even D: in a D‑regular graph with even D, 1‑way infinite bridges do not exist (degree‑sum parity on the finite side). Thus Theorem 14(ii) cannot extend to even D.

Meagreness of 2‑way infinite bridges (Theorem 16): full proof plan
Fix D > 2 and work in either G_{•,≤D} or G_{•,=D}.
Step 1 (nowhere dense at fixed distance). For k ∈ N, let P_k be the set “there exists a 2‑way infinite bridge at distance ≤ k from the root.” I claim P_k is nowhere dense.
- Let G_{•}^F be any realized cylinder. Consider the finite set E_k of edges of F at distance ≤ k from the root. Partition E_k into two classes: “safe” edges for which at least one side of B_r(F) − e has no boundary vertex in L_r(F) with residual degree (< D); and “critical” edges otherwise. For a safe edge e, in any extension within G_{•}^F, at least one side cannot become infinite (only boundary vertices can acquire outside neighbors), hence e cannot be a 2‑way infinite bridge in any such extension.
- For the set S of boundary vertices with residual degree, attach (outside the radius r) one new neighbor x_v to each v ∈ S and then connect the x_v’s into a single external cycle using new edges and vertices. This modification yields an extension H of F. For any critical e, there is at least one v_A ∈ S on one side of e and one v_B ∈ S on the other; inside F there are paths from each endpoint of e to v_A and v_B, respectively, not using e. In H, the external cycle then gives a path from v_A to v_B outside F, so e lies on a cycle inside some bounded radius R (e.g., R = r + 2), hence e is not a bridge in any completion that fixes B_R(H).
- Let F′ = B_R(H). Then G_{•}^{F′} ⊆ G_{•}^F and G_{•}^{F′} ∩ P_k = ∅, proving P_k is nowhere dense.
Step 2 (meagreness). The set P of graphs with a 2‑way infinite bridge equals ⋃_{k∈N} P_k and is thus meagre.
Step 3 (not nowhere dense; density). P is dense: from any cylinder G_{•}^F, choose a boundary vertex u with deg_F(u) < D and extend far outside to attach two disjoint infinite D‑bounded trees T_1, T_2 that are connected by a single edge e. Connect the core to exactly one of T_i by a long path disjoint from everything else. Then e is a 2‑way infinite bridge. By choosing the attachments beyond some radius R, we obtain a subcylinder G_{•}^{B_R} ⊆ G_{•}^F containing such graphs, and further refinements cannot destroy the already present 2‑way infinite bridge. Hence the closure of P has nonempty interior (indeed, P is dense meagre).
Consequences. In G_{•,=D} with even D, bridges are necessarily 2‑way infinite; therefore “has a bridge” is meagre, and the complement “bridgeless” is comeagre in the standard local topology. This complements Theorem 14 and should be recorded explicitly.

Critique and corrections for Theorem 14 and 15
- Theorem 14(i) (≤ D): Replace the pendant‑leaf argument with the saturated bubble lemma above. It yields, for every realized cylinder, a subcylinder all of whose members have a bridge, hence the bridgeless set is nowhere dense.
- Theorem 14(ii) (= D, odd D): Same correction via the D‑regular bubble. Parity prohibits 1‑way bridges for even D, explaining the exception.
- Theorem 15 (no cut vertex nowhere dense): For even D‑regular, provide an explicit local gadget that forces a cut vertex while keeping degrees D within the ball (e.g., two D‑regular “blobs” that only meet at a vertex v within the ball, with all degrees saturated there). Spell out: placement inside a clopen cylinder, degree saturation, and existence of D‑regular infinite completions along other boundary stubs. For ≤ D or odd D, this follows from the bridge‑forcing constructions (a bridge implies a cut vertex), but write this implication and the quantifiers explicitly.

Critique of Theorem 17 (edge‑chromatic number)
- The parity remark “removing an edge preserves χ′ = D+1” is false in general (e.g., K_3). To make χ′ = D + 1 nowhere dense, you need a finite class‑II forcer H_D: a finite D‑bounded graph such that any supergraph containing H_D as an induced subgraph satisfies χ′ ≥ D + 1. Candidates: for D odd, odd‑order D‑regular graphs; for D even, use Goldberg–Seymour or explicit overfull subgraphs. Then argue you can implant H_D inside any realized cylinder by attaching it outside the current radius and saturating degrees on the H_D side within the ball to block recoloring via outside edges. This needs a proper lemma; as written, Theorem 17 lacks a valid proof.

Topological sensitivity of typicality: what can and cannot change
- Nonmonotonicity caveat. Typicality (comeagreness) and nowhere denseness are not preserved under arbitrary topological refinements/coarsenings. Thus the monotonicity claims need to be removed. Any invariance statement must be proved for a specific class of topologies.
- Cylinder‑open topologies. Let τ be any topology on G_{•,D} that contains the local topology (equivalently, all local cylinders are τ‑open). Then the bridge‑forcing lemmas imply: for ≤ D and for = D with odd D, the set of bridgeless graphs is nowhere dense in τ as well (because every τ‑open set contains a local cylinder, which contains a subcylinder of all graphs with a bridge). Hence no such “natural refinement” makes bridgeless typical in these spaces.
- Even D‑regular: under the local topology, bridgeless is comeagre (using the 2‑way bridge meagreness above). Thus no metric change is needed to make bridgeless typical in this subspace.
- Coarser “natural” topologies: To flip typicality in ≤ D or odd D, one would need a coarser topology where the complement of the bridgeless set is meagre. But many natural coarsenings (e.g., quotienting by ends, or merging large annuli) still have cylinders as open sets or at least have an open base refining cylinders; for such topologies the forcing argument persists. A precise negative result could be proved for any “cylinder‑based” topology: a topology whose base consists of sets determined by finitely many local constraints around the root. Formalizing this (your “tame/cylinder‑rich” notions) and proving the invariance there would largely settle the conjecture negatively for a broad class of reasonable metrics.

Conclusion
