Metric facts and cylinders
- Off‑by‑one correction. For the local metric d_• defined by R=sup{r: B_r matches}, one has d_•((G,ρ),(H,σ))=2^{-R}. Thus d_•<2^{-r} iff B_{r+1} matches, and d_•≤2^{-r} iff B_r matches. The equivalence in the manuscript should be corrected to prevent radius slippage in cylinder refinements.
- Cylinders are clopen. For a rooted r‑ball F, C(F):=G_{•}^{F} is clopen (ultrametric). ND characterization: S⊆G_{•,D} is nowhere dense iff for every r‑ball F there exists r′ and F′ with C(F′)⊆C(F)\S. Use this uniformly in ND arguments.

Root‑invariance: what is solid and what to fix

- Remove: “Int(cl(S)) is saturated.” The given sketch is incorrect; rerooting does not preserve membership in a fixed cylinder, so this implication does not follow. It is also unnecessary for ND.
- Keep: Closure of saturated sets is saturated. Proof: If S is saturated and (G,ρ)∈cl(S), then for any u and t choose r≫dist(ρ,u)+t and (H_r,σ_r)∈S with B_r(H_r,σ_r)≅B_r(G,ρ). Let u_r be the image of u; saturation gives (H_r,u_r)∈S and B_t(H_r,u_r)≅B_t(G,u). Hence (G,u)∈cl(S).

Quotient/selector caveat (with counterexample)
- The forget‑the‑root map π need not send cylinders to open sets in any unrooted quotient grounded in the rooted topology: saturations of cylinders are typically not open. Example: let F be the 1‑ball of a leaf‑root; C(F)={root has degree 1}. Its saturation Sat(C(F))={has a leaf somewhere} is not open: in any cylinder around (H,σ) where the only leaf lies far outside the prescribed ball, one can delete that far leaf without changing the ball, yielding a point outside Sat(C(F)). Therefore π(C(F)) is not open; avoid arguments that require π(C) to be open. Work directly with cylinders.

Theorem 14 (bridgeless is nowhere dense): flaws and fixes
- Flaw: “attach a leaf” on the boundary does not force a bridge in every completion in Δ≤D; external edges from other boundary vertices can create a P–L path avoiding the candidate edge, killing its bridge status.
- Fix (Δ≤D): Use a finite “port” gadget within a refined ball that saturates all boundary vertices to degree D except a single port p which has exactly one free stub; connect a pendant path of suitable length to p so that the unique cross‑boundary edge in any completion is the edge at p. Then any completion has a cut‑edge at that port (a 1‑way infinite bridge). Provide the short proof: after removing that edge, the finite interior separates from the infinite exterior because all other boundary vertices have no free capacity.
- Fix (Δ= D): 1‑way bridges are impossible; instead force a 2‑way bridge. Build a finite core saturating all boundary degrees except two designated ports x,y with exactly one free stub each, and ensure x,y lie in different finite “cages” inside the ball. In any D‑regular completion, exactly one external edge at x and one at y must appear. Connect them outside by a single edge; then that edge is a 2‑way infinite bridge in any completion satisfying the cylinder. Provide the gadget and a completion lemma that D‑regular completions preserving the ball exist.

Theorem 16 (2‑way bridges are meagre but not ND): details and scope
- ND decomposition: For fixed k, let B_k be the set “there exists a 2‑way infinite bridge whose midpoint has distance ≤k from the root.” Given any cylinder C(F), refine to C(F′) by adding, within the refined ball, a short cycle through every edge at distance ≤k from the root (in Δ≤D, add a 2‑path and one extra edge; in Δ= D, saturate degrees internally and add degree‑preserving cycle gadgets). Then no such edge can be a bridge in any completion; hence B_k is nowhere dense.
- Density: Given any cylinder, complete arbitrarily and then, far beyond the prescribed ball, splice two infinite components and connect them by a single edge (Δ≤D). In Δ= D, first prepare two ports at a larger outer sphere (degree bookkeeping as in Theorem 14) and connect them by a single edge outside. Thus the union ⋃_k B_k is dense but meagre, and the property is not nowhere dense. Root‑invariance here is immediate since the set is saturated.

Ambiguity sets and meagre root‑invariance: caution and a corrected path
- Caution: The earlier plan to show Amb_k (“exists w within distance ≤k with two isomorphic neighbor‑rootings”) is nowhere dense can fail on cylinders with large interior symmetries (e.g., regular trees). Decorations “through a single boundary hub” cannot break interior symmetries without modifying the inner ball; hence that approach is invalid.
- Corrected strategy (outer‑shell rigidification, sketch): Given C(F) and k, choose a much larger radius R and attach, only at the outer sphere (dist=R), pairwise non‑isomorphic finite “signature trees” to the unique new neighbors of each boundary vertex so that (i) all degrees within dist<R are unchanged; (ii) the pointwise stabilizer of the outer sphere fixes the inner ball up to radius k; and (iii) for each w at dist≤k and u≠v∈N(w), the rooted neighborhoods (G,u) and (G,v) become non‑isomorphic in any completion. This shows Amb_k is nowhere dense under appropriate degree budgeting (Δ≤D straightforward; Δ=D needs port preparation). With a standard coverage of reroot‑saturations by countably many finite path moves, one can then deduce: saturation of a nowhere‑dense set is meagre on a comeagre domain (details still to be written carefully).
