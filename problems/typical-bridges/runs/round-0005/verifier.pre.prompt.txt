# Open Problem: Resolve the Final Two Conjectures on Typical Properties of Countable Graphs

This note poses two conjectures extracted from the extended abstract **‚ÄúTypical properties of countable graphs: flows and bridges (extended abstract)‚Äù** by Robert ≈†√°mal and Hadi Zamani (EUROCOMB‚Äô25). We first state the setting precisely and then formulate the conjectures. The authors‚Äô extended abstract (lightly re‚Äëtypeset) follows afterward for context.

---

## Setting

- Fix an integer \(D \ge 2\).
- Let \(\mathbf{G}_\bullet\) be the space of **countably infinite, connected, rooted graphs** \((G,\rho)\).
- For \((G,\rho) \in \mathbf{G}_\bullet\) and \(r \in \mathbb{N}\), let \(B_r(G,\rho)\) denote the rooted (closed) ball of radius \(r\) about \(\rho\).
- Equip \(\mathbf{G}_\bullet\) with the **local (Benjamini‚ÄìSchramm) metric** \(d_\bullet\) given by
  \[
  d_\bullet\big((G,\rho),(H,\sigma)\big)=2^{-R},
  \qquad
  R=\sup\{r\in\mathbb{N} : B_r(G,\rho)\cong B_r(H,\sigma)\}.
  \]
  (Equivalently, \(d_\bullet((G,\rho),(H,\sigma))<2^{-r}\) iff \(B_r(G,\rho)\cong B_r(H,\sigma)\).)
- Degree‚Äëconstrained subspaces:
  - \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,\le D}\): all rooted graphs with \(\Delta(G)\le D\).
  - \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,= D}\): all rooted graphs with \(\Delta(G)= D\).
  We write \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,D}\) for either of these when the distinction is unimportant.
- A subset \(S\subseteq \mathbf{G}_\bullet\) is **nowhere dense** if the interior of \(\overline{S}\) is empty. A set is **meagre** (first category) if it is a countable union of nowhere dense sets; its complement is **comeagre**. A property is called **typical** if the corresponding set is comeagre.
- An edge \(e\) of an (unrooted) graph \(G\) is a **bridge** if \(G-e\) has more connected components than \(G\). A bridge is **2‚Äëway infinite** if both components are infinite and **1‚Äëway infinite** otherwise.

## Conjectures

### Conjecture 1 ‚Äî Root‚Äëinvariance of smallness
Let \(P\) be any property of unrooted graphs and put
\[
S_P=\{(G,\rho)\in \mathbf{G}_{\bullet,D}: G\text{ has }P\}.
\]
Whether \(S_P\) is **nowhere dense** (respectively, **meagre**) in \((\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,D},d_\bullet)\) **does not depend on the choice of the root** \(\rho\).

### Conjecture 2 ‚Äî Metric sensitivity of typicality
There exists a **natural alternative metric** on the same underlying set \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,D}\) for which the collection of **typical properties** differs from those under \(d_\bullet\). In particular, determine whether there is a reasonable metric in which **bridgeless graphs are typical**, or prove that no such metric can change their typicality.

#### Abstract
We have two aims. **First**, we study nowhere‚Äëzero flows in countably infinite graphs, observing the different roles of two types of bridges. **Second**, we study how typical graphs with these types of bridges are. As there is no natural probability measure to use, we turn to an analytic approach: we study which sets are nowhere dense and which are meagre.

In finite graphs, bridges play a crucial role‚Äîit is easy to see that every flow assigns value \(0\) to every bridge. It is also known that graphs with a bridge are not typical: both dense random graphs and random regular graphs are bridgeless w.h.p.¬†[3,‚ÄØ1]. We ask how this transfers to countable graphs. In Section¬†1 we explain differences between bridges and flows and, via compactness, we prove a version of Seymour‚Äôs 6‚Äëflow theorem for countable graphs.

In Section¬†2 we explore nowhere‚Äëdense and meagre sets. This is called for because the usual probabilistic models collapse: extending Erd≈ës‚ÄìR√©nyi to countably infinite graphs yields the Rado graph a.s., so we are effectively studying a single graph up to isomorphism. Attempting random regular graphs via the configuration model [1] would require a uniform distribution on perfect matchings of a countable set (e.g., neighbors of the first vertex), which does not exist. Consequently, we turn to Baire category: meagre/co‚Äëmeagre sets (also ‚Äúfirst/second category‚Äù).

### 1. Nowhere‚Äëzero flows for infinite graphs

**Definition¬†1 (weak flow).**  
Given an orientation \(D\) of a graph \(G\) and an abelian group \(A\), a **weak \(A\)‚Äëflow** is a function \(\varphi:E(G)\to A\) such that **Kirchhoff‚Äôs law for each vertex cut** holds:
\[
\forall v\in V(G):\quad \sum_{e\in \delta^-(v)}\varphi(e)=\sum_{e\in \delta^+(v)}\varphi(e).
\]

**Definition¬†2 (strong flow).**  
Given \(D,G,A\) as above, a **strong \(A\)‚Äëflow** is a function \(\varphi:E(G)\to A\) such that **Kirchhoff‚Äôs law for each cut** holds:
\[
\forall S\subseteq V(G):\quad \sum_{e\in \delta^+(S)}\varphi(e)=\sum_{e\in \delta^-(S)}\varphi(e).
\]

In either case, \(\varphi\) is a **nowhere‚Äëzero** weak/strong \(A\)‚Äëflow if \(\varphi^{-1}(0)=\varnothing\).

**Definition¬†3 (bridge).**  
An edge \(e\) of \(G\) is a **bridge** if deleting \(e\) increases the number of connected components. A bridge is **2‚Äëway infinite** if both resulting components are infinite, and **1‚Äëway infinite** otherwise.

Every strong flow is a weak flow. While the two notions are equivalent on finite graphs, they diverge on infinite graphs: e.g., on a two‚Äëway infinite path, a nowhere‚Äëzero weak \(A\)‚Äëflow exists but a strong one does not. In¬†[6] the authors construct a 3‚Äëconnected graph with two ends that admits a nowhere‚Äëzero weak \(A\)‚Äëflow but no nowhere‚Äëzero strong \(A\)‚Äëflow. Nearly all finite flow theorems extend to infinite **strong** \(A\)‚Äëflows via topological compactness; here we use **logical** compactness to obtain:

**Theorem¬†4.**  
*A countably infinite, locally finite graph without a 1‚Äëway infinite bridge has a nowhere‚Äëzero weak 6‚Äëflow.*

### 2. Metric space of rooted connected graphs

Since no good probability measure exists for countable graphs, we study typicality via a metric topology and Baire category.

**Definition¬†5 (Œµ‚Äëneighborhood).**  
For a metric space \((X,d)\) and \(x\in X\), the Œµ‚Äëneighborhood is \(U_\varepsilon(x)=\{y\in X:\ d(x,y)<\varepsilon\}\). The closed neighborhood \(\overline{U}_\varepsilon(x)\) uses \(d(x,y)\le \varepsilon\).

**Definition¬†6 (open set).**  
A set \(U\subseteq X\) is open if \(\forall x\in U\ \exists \varepsilon>0:\ U_\varepsilon(x)\subseteq U\).

We now fix the graph space and metric.

**Definition¬†9 (space of rooted graphs).**  
Let \(\mathbf{G}_\bullet\) be the set of all connected rooted graphs (up to root‚Äëpreserving isomorphism). For \(D>2\), let \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,\le D}\) be those with all degrees \(\le D\), and \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,=D}\) those with all degrees \(=D\).

**Definition¬†10 (r‚Äëball).**  
For \((G,v)\), the rooted r‚Äëball is
\[
B_r(G,v)=(\,G[\{y\in V(G): d(v,y)\le r\}],\,v\,).
\]

**Definition¬†11 (metric \(d_\bullet\)).**  
For \((G_1,v_1),(G_2,v_2)\in\mathbf{G}_\bullet\),
\[
d_\bullet\big((G_1,v_1),(G_2,v_2)\big)=\inf\{2^{-r} : B_r(G_1,v_1)\cong B_r(G_2,v_2)\}.
\]
This is a metric¬†[5].

**Definition¬†12 (extensions of an r‚Äëball).**  
For an r‚Äëball \(F=B_r(G,v)\), define
\[
\mathbf{G}_\bullet^F=\{(G',v')\in \mathbf{G}_\bullet : B_r(G',v')\cong F\}.
\]

**Lemma¬†13 (basic facts in \((\mathbf{G}_\bullet,d_\bullet)\)).**  
- For each \((G,x)\), \(r\in\mathbb{N}\), and \(0<\varepsilon<1\):
  \[
  U_{2^{-r}+\varepsilon}((G,x))=U_{2^{-r}}((G,x))=\mathbf{G}_\bullet^{B_r(G,x)}.
  \]
- \(\mathbf{G}_\bullet^F\) is nonempty if \(F\) is an r‚Äëball in \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,\le D}\) that contains a vertex of degree \< \(D\) at distance \(r\) from the root.
- A subset \(S\subseteq \mathbf{G}_\bullet\) is nowhere dense iff
  \(\forall\) r‚Äëballs \(F\ \exists\) r‚Ä≤‚Äëball \(F'\) with \(\mathbf{G}_\bullet^{F'}\subseteq \mathbf{G}_\bullet^{F}\setminus S\).
- For all \(D>2\), \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,D}\) is not meagre.

### Main results on typical properties

**Theorem¬†14.**  
*The set of bridgeless graphs is nowhere dense (i) in \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,\le D}\) for all \(D>2\); and (ii) in \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,=D}\) for all odd \(D>2\).*

**Theorem¬†16.**

We also considered other (a)typical properties:

**Theorem¬†17.**

### 3. Proof sketches

**Proof of Theorem¬†4 (sketch).**  
Let the vertices of \(G\) be \(v_1,v_2,\ldots\) and orient each edge \(\{v_i,v_j\}\) from the smaller to the larger index. For each radius \(r\), augment the r‚Äëball \(B_r(G,v_1)\) to a finite multigraph \(G_r\) by adding a sink vertex \(v_\infty\) and connecting boundary edges to \(v_\infty\). Introduce Boolean variables \(F_{e,1},\dots,F_{e,6}\) encoding which of six nonzero values an edge \(e\) receives, enforce exclusivity \(\bigvee_i F_{e,i}\) and \(\bigwedge_{j\ne i}\neg F_{e,j}\), and write linear parity constraints at each vertex (Kirchhoff‚Äôs law). Let \(F_{G_r}\) be the conjunction over vertices of \(G_r\); then every finite subset of the constraint family \(\{F_{G_r}\}_r\cup\{F_{e,i}\}_e\) is satisfiable (by [7]), hence by compactness [4] the whole set is satisfiable, yielding a nowhere‚Äëzero weak 6‚Äëflow on \(G\).

**Proof idea for Theorem¬†14.**  
Given an r‚Äëball \(F\), construct an r‚Ä≤‚Äëball \(F'\) such that every extension of \(F'\) contains a bridge. For \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,\le D}\), attach a leaf to a boundary vertex of degree \<\(D\). For \(\mathbf{G}_{\bullet,=D}\) handle regularity via parity gadgets: define \(S(F)=\sum_{v\in L_r}(D-\deg v)\) where \(L_r\) is the boundary; use \(K_{D+1}-e\) gadgets and tree‚Äëlike expansions to force parity \(S(F)=1\), then attach a new vertex to create a bridge. Hence the bridgeless set is nowhere dense.

**Proof idea for Theorem¬†15.**  
In even \(D\)‚Äëregular graphs, use gadgets to force a cut vertex; in odd \(D\) or degree‚Äëbounded spaces, this also follows from the previous construction.

**Proof idea for Theorem¬†16.**  
Being ‚Äúthere is a 2‚Äëway infinite bridge at distance \(k\)‚Äù is nowhere dense; take a union over \(k\in\mathbb{N}\) to get a meagre (but not nowhere‚Äëdense) set.

**Proof idea for Theorem¬†17.**  
For each \(D\), there exist \(D\)‚Äëregular graphs with \(\chi'(G)=D+1\) (even \(D\): complete graphs or any odd‚Äëorder \(D\)‚Äëregular graph; odd \(D\): by Tutte‚Äôs theorem). Removing an edge preserves \(\chi'=D+1\) by parity. Embed parity gadgets within r‚Äëballs so that every extension has edge‚Äëchromatic number \(D+1\), hence the set is nowhere dense.
